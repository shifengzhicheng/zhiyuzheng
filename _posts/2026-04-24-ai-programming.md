# From Traditional Programming to AI-Assisted Programming and Research

## Introduction

Looking back to 2022, the first time I truly felt the power of AI was when [programmers used Stable Diffusion to outperform human artists in illustration](https://static.zhoudaosh.com/3ADA67F46F70D22FF899EAC26C00AFCD7DD731A86619D296C70B66A1CA0BCB93). At that time, many argued that AI-generated works lacked a "soul." However, I believed that art simply serves as an informational database accumulated by human civilization.

That same winter, GPT-3.5 emerged. Although criticized for lacking deep logic, it felt incredibly novel. It could quickly help me implement unfamiliar syntax or libraries. Despite occasional bugs, it genuinely solved problems I only partially understood.

Since then, AI's rapid development has felt unstoppable. With continuous iterations and optimizations, it seems we may have already crossed a technological singularity. In the near future, human work might be reduced to interface construction and data calibration, while generation and idea verification are handled by AI. The era of struggling to acquire knowledge is closing; soon, AI may replace humans in active creation—not because humans can't create, but because relying on AI will simply be more cost-effective.

## Before: What Traditional Programming Taught Me

I feel fortunate that before the widespread adoption of AI, I experienced the complete cycle of traditional programming: compiling, writing code, designing data structures, and debugging. This foundation gave me a profound understanding of the computer world. Beyond merely knowing what source code does, I understand how programs are compiled, linked, and executed at the architecture level.

I am skeptical that relying entirely on "vibe coding" without foundational knowledge can lead to efficient implementations or sustainable long-term projects. While AI's context window is vast, it often drifts from the main objective. Because the ultimate goals are still defined by humans, blindly executing AI's suggestions without original thought is a highly risky approach.

True innovation stems from connecting our understanding of the real world with the fundamental mechanics of the computer world. In AI-assisted programming, foundational knowledge remains essential, and genuine curiosity will always be your most reliable guide.

## The Turning Point: AI Tools Entering My Workflow

AI一开始只是作为一个toy tool的存在，我会去问它一些简单的问题，并让它帮我理解简单的文章，分析代码片段，生成简单的代码。它作为一个生成式的工具，能够快速地提供一些初步的解决方案或者思路，这让我在面对一些不熟悉的问题时能够更快地找到解决问题的方向。我不会完全依赖它来完成复杂的任务，而是把它当作一个辅助工具，帮助我更快地获取信息以辅助我解决问题。

## Now: AI Programming as Collaboration, Not Replacement

This section can explain my current understanding of AI-assisted programming:

- AI is closer to a fast-feedback collaborator than a tool that completes everything automatically;
- humans still need to define problems, decompose tasks, and judge results;
- writing a good prompt is also a form of engineering communication;
- the center of programming shifts partly from line-by-line implementation to problem modeling, verification, and system design.

## Toward Research: From Writing Code to Asking Questions

This section can shift the focus from programming to research:

- traditional programming often focuses on making things work, while research asks whether a problem is worth studying;
- AI can help read papers, organize ideas, and generate experimental scripts, but it cannot replace research judgment;
- the core abilities in research are still asking questions, designing experiments, and interpreting results;
- AI-assisted programming lowers the cost of iteration and makes it faster to test ideas.

## How My Workflow Has Changed

This comparison can make the shift easier to understand:

| Before | Now |
| --- | --- |
| Search for references first, then write code manually | Ask AI to help map the direction, then filter and verify manually |
| Implement features line by line | Describe goals, constraints, and interfaces before reviewing generated code |
| Debug mainly through personal experience | Combine logs, tests, and AI-assisted analysis to locate issues |
| Write documentation after implementation | Record explanations and decisions during development |

## Risks I Still Need to Watch

This section can be more direct and honest:

- AI can generate content that looks reasonable but is actually wrong;
- over-reliance on AI may weaken sensitivity to fundamentals;
- in research, "it runs" should not be confused with "it contributes";
- generated results still need verification, reproduction, and review.

## What I Want to Do Next

Possible next steps:

- build a more stable AI-assisted research workflow;
- connect paper reading, implementation, and experiment logging;
- test the limits of AI-assisted programming in concrete research problems;
- summarize prompts, templates, and experiment practices that fit my own workflow.

## Closing Thoughts

This section can close the article with the main takeaway:

AI-assisted programming does not make traditional programming skills unnecessary. Instead, it makes me more aware that the real challenge is defining problems, organizing systems, verifying results, and turning vague ideas into research work that can run, be reproduced, and be discussed.
